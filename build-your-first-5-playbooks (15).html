Build Your First 5 Playbooks in Console

This guide walks you through building five playbooks yourself, from Site-based wifi support to a fully autonomous security response, so you can gain a deep understanding of how you can use playbooks to automate your operational tasks.

How playbooks work

Playbooks help you automate your operational tasks, both fully autonomously, and with human in the loop checkpoints, depending on your workflow. This means only the requests that actually need human touch land in your queue. To build your playbooks, you can simply navigate to Assistant and describe your most common requests and the outcome you want in natural language. Assistant will then figure out the rest: which actions to take, when to take those actions, and where a human checkpoint might belong.

Before we get into building our first playbook together, let's look at the four parts of a playbook.

01

Trigger Type

The event that starts the playbook. This could be a Slack or Teams message, a pre-set time/date/frequency, or a Webhook from another connected system.

02

Conditions

How Console decides which playbook to run. You will want to check that Assistant has written the Conditions specific enough to avoid false matches, and general enough to catch the different ways people may phrase a request.

03

Instructions

What Console does once triggered, written in natural language — #Tool:Action calls an action in a connected system, @Resource points to a document or channel.

04

Testing

Runs a test flow of your playbook before publishing to confirm that the right playbook is being run at the right time, and that the workflow is working as intended. You will learn various troubleshooting techniques as you build out the five playbooks in this guide.

A few important points to keep in mind as you build

There are a few requirements for every playbook to run, and a few that are required depending on the playbook you're trying to build.

Required for every playbook
  • Integrations connected — Playbooks can only take actions through connected apps (Settings > Integrations). If nothing off-the-shelf fits, you can build a custom action instead.
  • Console added to your Slack channels — Console needs to be a member of any channel where it'll listen for requests.
Only if your playbook needs it
  • Knowledge base connected — only if you're citing @Article. Sync Confluence, Notion, Google Docs, etc. first.
  • Approval flows, categories, or escalation config — set these up if your playbook requires human sign-off, classification, or routing.
  • Scope decided — can this playbook act on the requester's own resources only, or does it need elevated scope to act on someone else's? Should it be restricted to certain users?

One thing worth knowing: Console knows a requester's email, name, title, department, and device info (if MDM is connected) — but not group membership. If a playbook depends on group membership, look it up in the instructions or restrict who can run it.

With this in mind, let's build our first playbook.

Playbook 1: Site-based wifi support

Let's build our first playbook (Site-based wifi support).

Operational goal

Automatically deflect requests from teammates in the different offices around the country who are having trouble connecting to the wifi.

A note before we start: This playbook will search through our Knowledge Base to surface Wifi Passwords depending on location.

If you don't already have Knowledge Base articles built for Wifi troubleshooting, you can either create ones that apply to your company, or create a few test knowledge base articles to build this playbook:

  1. Navigate to Knowledge Base.
  2. Create and publish three articles:
    • San Francisco Wifi — Content: Password 1234
    • Manila Wifi — Content: Password 4321
    • Boston Wifi — Content: Password 5678
Example prompt
"When someone in [Insert Slack Channel] asks how to connect to or is having issues with their wifi, search our Knowledge Base for the matching article. Because we have multiple office locations, be sure to ask the employee which office they're in before responding. Then share the password with them (or troubleshooting steps), and include the article link."
Playbook 02 / 05

New Hire Slack Onboarding

Operational goal

Automatically add new hires to the right Slack channels based on their Okta groups (or HR department as a fallback), and send them a welcome message — no manual channel-adding required.

A note before we start: For this playbook, Console will read through a Google Sheet which maps Okta groups to the channels they should be added to. Be sure to have the location where you store your group/channel mapping handy to share with Assistant as it builds. Or, just ask Assistant to add in a placeholder where you can go back and add the location once you have it!
[VIDEO — Building Playbook 2 live, start to finish]
Example prompt
"Please build a playbook that automatically onboards new hires into Slack. When someone joins, it should add them to the right Slack channels based on the teams/groups they're in, and send them a welcome message. For figuring out which channels, I keep a mapping in a [Google Sheet] (group → Slack channel). I'd like it to look at the person's Okta groups first, and fall back to their department/team from [HR Software e.g. HiBob] if there's no match. It should trigger off Okta when someone gets added to a group, look up the matching channel in the Sheet, add them, and send a welcome note — just once, not for every group. If something fails, open a ticket for IT."
Things to keep in mind

Your HR system's department or team field needs to match the naming used in your Google Sheet mapping — if HR lists a team as "Eng" but the Sheet expects "Engineering," the fallback lookup will silently fail to find a channel. Whatever HR software you use, confirm the naming lines up before relying on it.

Playbook 03 / 05

Seat Usage Cleanup

Operational goal

Automatically detect and reclaim inactive software licenses to optimize IT SaaS spend and maintain security hygiene.

A note before we start: For this playbook, Console will automatically Slack employees every month to see who is using their Salesforce account. Employees can respond yes/no, which will determine if Console deactivates their account. Think about:
  • Which software you want to check on — and is it connected to Console, or should you leave a placeholder?
  • How often do you want Console to check in with employees?
  • What do you want to happen if they don't respond?
  • Consider groups of people you may want to omit from this monthly check (e.g. C-Suite).
[VIDEO — Building Playbook 3 live, start to finish]
Example prompt
"Every 30 days, check Salesforce for users who haven't logged in during that window. Ask each one in Slack if they still need their license. If they say no, or don't respond within 48 hours, remove them from the app's Okta group. Exclude anyone with an Executive profile. Post a summary of what was reclaimed to #it-finance-audit."
Things to keep in mind

Exclusion rules like "Executive profile" are easy to add but easy to forget about later — if your org's exemption list grows beyond one profile type, revisit this playbook rather than letting exceptions pile up as one-off manual overrides.

The 48-hour non-response default counts as "no." Make sure that's the behavior you actually want — some teams prefer defaulting to "keep" on non-response for tools tied to compliance or client-facing work.

Playbook 04 / 05

Travel Access

Operational goal

Manage temporary access for employees logging in from restricted geographic locations — auto-approving short, low-risk requests while routing longer requests for human review.

A note before we start: For this playbook, we have specific policies in place for international travel and laptop usage (e.g. employees cannot use their laptops outside of the US). As you build, be sure to consider your own company's policies for laptop/VPN/Wifi usage in different geographic regions (and what types of approvals you can automate).
[VIDEO — Building Playbook 4 live, start to finish]
Example prompt
"When someone asks for temporary laptop access while they're traveling abroad, first pull their recent login location from the Okta system log, then send them a form asking where they're going, how long they'll be gone, and why. If the trip is 5 days or less, just auto-approve it and add them to the Travel-Laptop-Access Okta group for exactly the dates they're traveling so it turns off on its own. If it's longer than that, post in #it-security-alerts and wait for someone to approve before granting — and if nobody replies within 15 minutes, Slack DM the on-call admin. Either way, tie the access to the travel window so it revokes automatically when they're back."
Things to keep in mind

The 5-day threshold should match your own org's travel or security policy — don't treat it as a fixed rule.

Auto-approval paths carry more risk than approval-gated ones, since there's no human checkpoint. Consider whether the auto-approved path should still notify #it-security-alerts for visibility, even without requiring their sign-off.

People miss Slack messages. The 15-minute escalation only covers the manual-approval path — make sure your on-call rotation is actually staffed for security escalations, not just general IT ones.

Playbook 05 / 05

Device Isolation

Operational goal

Instantly isolate a compromised endpoint and revoke user sessions the moment an automated malware download is detected by your security monitoring software.

A note before we start: This is a more high-stakes playbook where we isolate what may be a compromised device. For this playbook, you can either connect your current EDR platform or ask Assistant to leave a system placeholder where it calls the EDR platform if you're still working on the connection.
[VIDEO — Building Playbook 5 live, start to finish]
Example prompt
"When our EDR platform (CrowdStrike or SentinelOne) flags a confirmed high-severity malware or ransomware alert on a corporate device, we identify the assigned user from the device metadata and immediately isolate the host from the network. We then revoke the user's active sessions and expire their password to shut down anyone who may have hijacked the account. Finally, an override form capturing the incident status, host remediation action, and responding analyst's sign-off is posted to #security-war-room, and the on-call security lead is paged at the same time."
Things to keep in mind

Automated network isolation can accidentally take down a critical developer server or a leadership endpoint during an important customer meeting if a benign file triggers a false positive. Embedding a high-visibility, single-click "Incident Isolation Override" form directly inside the Slack security channel ensures your team can instantly reverse the network block the moment a false alarm is verified.

Should every device be automatically isolatable, or are some (leadership, production servers) exempt or routed to manual review first?

Who's authorized to submit the isolation override — any security team member, or a specific on-call role?

Should the on-call lead be paged for every isolation, or only ones that stay isolated past a certain point?

Does a malware alert always justify clearing sessions and expiring the password, or are there cases where you'd isolate the device without locking out the user?

How confident does the EDR alert need to be before this fires automatically — does "high-severity" from your specific tool actually mean "confirmed"?

Bonus: use Assistant to identify top playbooks to build

Assistant has full visibility into all of your support requests. This means it can identify top contenders for playbooks to build. You can prompt it right from within the Assistant page, and even give it certain criteria (e.g. volume vs. blockers) to filter through when creating a list of playbook opportunities. The video below will walk you through how to use Assistant to identify the next 4 playbooks you should build.

  1. Navigate to Assistant.
  2. Ask it to give you the most common requests in the past 5, 10, or 15 days: "what are the most common requests we've gotten in the past 15 days"
  3. Ask it to prioritize this list by quick wins: "Which requests would make the best quick wins playbooks? give me 4"
  4. Use this list to guide your next four playbooks to deflect even more requests.

That's it — you now know how to let Assistant surface your next set of playbook opportunities any time you need fresh ideas.

What's next

Great job! You've now built your first few playbooks, and this is just the start of your playbooks journey! Now that you understand the fundamentals of building a playbook, continue to look for those requests that hit your backlog most often to start.

Automating an ask that happens 100 times a month will provide quick relief while you tackle more complex flows!

Or, better yet, use Assistant to identify these playbook opportunities for you! Check out the "Bonus: use Assistant to identify top playbooks to build" page right before this one to see how.

Keep these in mind as you continue to build playbooks

  • You can only call what's connected. Okta and Slack get connected during onboarding, but several of these playbooks need more — HiBob, Salesforce, CrowdStrike or SentinelOne, PagerDuty. Ensure you have the right applications connected before as you build.
  • Always test before you publish. Get in the habit of testing every playbook before it goes live, not just the complex ones.
  • Watch for duplicate or conflicting sources. If a knowledge base has multiple versions of the same policy, Console will surface all of them. Archive or tag outdated docs so it references a single source of truth.
  • A playbook does something — it doesn't just describe something. A good playbook searches, decides, and acts.
